The Duke of Edinburgh Award (Georgina)

The Duke of Edinburgh Award (DoE) was started by Prince Philip on the 1st of September 1956 in the United Kingdom. It started as a bridge between school and service for boys, in which they undertook 4 sections; rescue and public service, expeditions, pursuits and projects, and fitness. The duke hoped that the programme would support, help and guide young men to gain valuable skills. The pilot of the DoE was so popular, that overseas programmes for girls were also introduced and the overall programme quickly grew popular.

The DoE is a set of challenges for young people between the ages of 14-24 that aims to create and develop transferable skills such as teamwork and social interaction in young people that can help them become well-rounded people who can function in professional environments.

🏕️

Tom’s DofE experience: “For my gold DofE, I was with a group of 5 others, and together we planned, mapped, prepped and walked a five day hike in Eryri National Park (Snowdonia), including summiting Yr Wyddfa (Snowdon). In many ways, the group teamwork and values that were used on said expedition were present in the group work for the tour, with the preparation, planning and execution.Now over 130 countries and territories have DoE programmes. Even during the pandemic 330000 young people still got involved in the DoE volunteering in their local communities. Most recently in 2023, the DoE has been passed onto the son of Prince Philip (Prince Edward, the new Duke of Edinburgh).”

It is divided into 4 sections:

  • Volunteering - aims to make young people look for meaningful opportunities in their local environment that make a difference rather than aiming to make money. For example, working for charities or non-profit organisations, social action causes, and within their school.
  • Physical - aims to make young people broaden their horizons, it can't be a physical activity, however, they can research information about a physical activity. For example, learning first aid skills, cooking, playing an instrument and learning to drive.
  • Skills and expedition - for the expedition section a young person will have to individually plan and complete an expedition with an agreed aim. They must do correct training and preparation for the expedition including a practice expedition. Then they must do the self-reliant expedition and then a presentation. This can include various modes of transport(not motorised) for example, on foot, bicycle, kayak/canoe, or horseback.
  • Residential (Gold level) - a young person does a shared activity or course away from home, possibly including both evenings and days. They must do it in a completely new environment with people they don't know previously. Should last a minimum of 4 nights spent away from home.

The DoE is split into 3 levels of awards- bronze, silver and gold. The bronze award is completed over a period of 6 months, the silver is also a 6-month timescale, and the gold lasts 12 months.

Equipment needed for the expedition section includes; clothes(walking boots, comfortable and waterproof clothes), a rucksack, a sleeping bag and mat, torch, first aid kit, food and water, cutlery, washkit, matches, a tent, stove, cooking utensils, map and compass.

The Area of Sidcup & Looking at DofE-style Maps (Emma)

It appears that the natural geography of Sidcup and the surrounding area - still including much greenery and park space to this day - has always been a highlight of the area, as the place-name ‘Sidcup’ is thought to be derived from ’Cetecopp,’ which means meaning ‘seat shaped or flat topped hill’. The first recorded use of this name was 1254. These days, with the many parks and open spaces that remain, the area hints at the great estates and large homes which once stood there. The Independent described how “The Sidcup site is in exquisite 18th-century parkland with squirrels, foxes, swans and a lake.”

When looking at how Sidcup and the area around it originated, early records show that it used to be a small hamlet on the road from Maidstone to London. The owner of this Hamlet "in the 35th year of king Henry VI. [i.e. in the 1450s] as appears by his deed." (Hasted, 1797) was called ‘Thomas de Sedcopp’. Towards the end of the 18th century, it then expanded to become "a small street of houses, among which is an inn of much resort" (Hasted, 1797).

Sidcup has continually expanded, and in the present day includes a mix of large Victorian and Edwardian properties, alongside typical 1930s suburbia buildings.

In terms of the specific Rose Bruford College campus, this was first used as an educational location when the Kent Education Committee offered to lease Rose Elizabeth Bruford Lamorbey House in 1950. (When she established the college). At the time of leasing, it was a Grade II listed manor house, going for £5 per year. Since this, the site has grown, with the majority of new buildings being completed in 2002.

One of the props we wanted to include in our tour was maps, and therefore Emma found it important to research what actual maps (such as the ones used on Dofe) look like and include. She used the article ‘How to use edofe mapping’ from the ‘Aspen Outdoors’ website to get references (Fig. 4-6) for the colours, patterns, shapes etc. This was also helpful to find out how certain geographical elements like parks and rivers were drawn.

The Glade by Lamorbey Park (Georgina)

The lake bordering Rose Bruford College and Lamorbey Park is best known as the glade. The glade is not a natural spring, it is derived from the river shuttle(on Avery Hill) which was dammed to create smaller waterways, which are located across the golf course. A plan made in 1761 was made to show the river shuttle being diverted into 3 smaller lakes. The glade was once used as an area for swimming when Lamorbey House was a hotel and many locals often swam there. The lake itself has also been used for fishing throughout the years, even now it is home to Lamorbey angling society.

The glade has become naturalised over the last 20 years, it now supports multiple wildlife species, including eels and water voles. The park itself has very diverse wildlife despite its small size having over 105 bird species registered there. The area surrounding the lake has an established forest of multiple species of trees. Unfortunately, the lake has a medium to high flood risk during the winter times.

Making Props & Sound for the Tour

New Re-Branded DofE Documents (Emma)

The first thing we thought might be a good nod to our audience was to have a DofE ‘reception’ bit at the start of our tour where we would take attendance of the ‘campers’. For this reception area, we wanted to have a DofE logo, however one that was rebranded to suit our ‘Rose Bruford Award’ idea (Fig. 2). Emma took on the task of creating this logo, using the app ‘Procreate’ on her Ipad. The first thing she did was find a normal DofE logo, which she put as a bottom layer on the drawing. Then, she used Dafont.com to find fonts similar to the ones used in the original logo. These ended up being the ‘Yorkie’ font for the main ‘R,B’ letters, and the ‘Cheese Pizza’ font for the smaller caption at the bottom. By overlaying the new letters onto the old logo (Fig. 1), she was able to match the size and style to make it look like an official new logo. As an extra touch, Emma replaced the crown at the top with a rose, a nod to the ‘founder’ of our award.

To make the tour more interesting and create more audience involvement, we also thought it might be fun to include completion certificates, as well as a proper register for the start (Fig. 5). For the certificate, Emma once again used an original DofE document as a base, and then layered text, drawings and other elements on top to change it to the new Rose Bruford theme (Fig. 4). She used the article ‘Famous People Of Sidcup : Rose Bruford and Doreen Bird’ from the Sidcup Spotlight Autumn 2022 to look up Rose’s full name, as well as birth date etc.

For the register, Emma also googled what different school registers could look like, and ended up going with a simple table which included the audience members’ names already (Fig. 3), looked up using a TEX411 Register that included the group numbers.

Maps for the Audience (Emma)

Due to the brief including the point of giving the audience an item/prop to identify them as the audience, we thought it might be fun if we had a collection of maps we could hand out at the start of the tour for them to follow. To make these maps, Emma first looked up an areal view of the Rose Bruford campus, simply using google maps (Fig. 1). Using the app Procreate on Ipad, she placed the screenshot of the site on the bottom layer, and then added various layers to draw over the original map. To make sure the style of the drawing somewhat emulated that of an actual map one might get on DofE, Emma looked up various DofE maps on the ‘Aspen Outdoors’ website. Through using different maps as reference, she was able to take inspiration from the colours, thickness of the lines, what was included and what wasn’t etc.

After finishing the drawing (Fig. 2), she went and printed around 30 copies of the map (to make sure each audience member was guaranteed one). The paper Emma used was A3, as this size wasn’t too small but also not too big for people to carry around during the tour. Each map was then folded in the style of a classic navigation map (Fig. 3).

“I started the Q-lab by working out which sound effects were needed at each stage of the tour, for example at the bush part of the tour there were bird sound effects.

I found the sound effects on the BBC sound effects library, I started at the bush with a fire sound effect as that group talked about fire safety and burning things. I added a low pass filter on a couple of the sound effects to make them sound less sharp and more natural. Also, I put the sound effects in groups (where they were located in the tours) so that they were more organised and easier to find and follow.”

Tom

“Of the two TEX411 projects so far, I have found this one the most enjoyable and interesting. The project overall was quite fun to put together and present, however I feel like I would’ve struggled on either of the two other briefs. One of the main reasons I liked this brief is that I have achieved the DofE awards, and I enjoyed the acting elements (especially getting to stand in the lake).

Overall, I think the group did a good job following the given brief, except potentially not linking the tour enough to Bruford. Incorporating a chronological narrative about the college, as there wasn’t much inspiration taken from the legacy or history of it, could have enhanced the content of the tour, as it was very much DofE focussed. However, I do feel that the group collaborated effectively when planning, researching, and executing the tour, from sharing different initial ideas to helping each other with the performances. This being a tour, we included areas of the campus, such as the lake, to give more meaning to the discussed narrative. We also made use of props and costumes throughout the tour, relating to the “Rose Bruford award”. The group members all had different roles to play, such as Izzy’s teacher persona and  Emma & Dom being troublemaking group members. We tried to use a soundscape in Dom’s rucksack, however it didn’t end up working as you couldn’t really hear it. To make the audience feel more included in the tour, we started with a “safety briefing” and gave them items used on DofE, such as maps and compasses, along with making the audience navigate around the campus themselves.  We also had some members of the group integrated into the audience, who roleplayed as people doing DofE, often creating trouble.

I found the exhibition at 180 studios to be shallow and lacking any substantial or meaningful throughline. Despite the interesting technical aspects of the pieces, there was no engaging or thought-provoking content to be found - the artist decided to give the heavy lifting of finding meaning in the pieces to the minds of the viewers. As for the comedy club, I liked the underground and close-up nature of the venue (disregarding the horrific cable management), and I found the comedians to be (for the most part) funny.

There are two possible interpretations for the purpose of the places visited in London this week, the first of which being that the trip was intended to influence the creative decisions for the making of the tours. To take this one into consideration, I don’t believe that either event could be used to influence the tour, not only because they don’t seem to have much connection to the subject matter, but they were only visited the day before the final presentation, leaving limited time to implement ideas collected in London. The alternative intention was to use them as a means to provoke critical reflection of the places visited, to which I would say that better venue choices could’ve been made. One possible example could’ve been Sunset Boulevard - the new showing of the classic musical in the West End could give a good starting point for many discussions relating more closely to our disciplines, as it has a very different and interesting take from the original showing.

Although we managed to incorporate some of the technical disciplines, such as CP or SA, into the tour, there was nothing in the project which allowed me to use or expand my field of knowledge, that being lighting. I believe that a similar problem exists in the cabinet of curiosities, in which we spend a week collecting and organising objects, instead of doing something more productive that relates to our courses. While I understand that the skills we are covering are useful, I believe that they can be presented in such a way that the technical aspects of the respective courses are integrated better into the projects.”
